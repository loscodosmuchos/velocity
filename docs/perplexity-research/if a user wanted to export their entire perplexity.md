# if a user wanted to export their entire perplexity library of apps to back it up or reference it as a whole/searchable, how would they do that?

While you cannot export your entire Perplexity library in a single action, you can export individual threads and search your library to find specific information.[^1_1][^1_2]

### Exporting Individual Threads

You can export the answer to a thread in several formats, including PDF, Markdown, or DOCX. This feature allows you to save important conversations or research for offline use or sharing. To do this, you would navigate to the specific thread and use the "Export" button. For users of Perplexity Labs, the same export functionality is available for Labs threads.[^1_3][^1_4][^1_1]

### Searching Your Library

Your Perplexity library is searchable by keywords or titles, allowing you to locate specific threads. To search your library, go to the Library page and use the search bar provided.[^1_2]

### Data Retention and Backup

Perplexity has different data retention policies depending on how and where files are stored.[^1_5]

* **Files attached to threads** are automatically deleted after a certain period (7 days for Enterprise Pro users and 30 days for other users).[^1_4][^1_5]
* **Files uploaded to your Organization's file repository, "My Files," or Spaces** are retained until you manually delete them.[^1_5]

Given that there is no bulk export feature, creating a comprehensive backup of your entire library would require exporting each thread individually.[^1_1]

Perplexity does not automatically name threads.[^2_1]

### Key Terminology and Concepts

* **Threads**: These are the core conversational spaces where you interact with Perplexity. Each thread maintains the context of the conversation, allowing for iterative problem-solving and in-depth exploration of a topic.[^2_2][^2_1]
* **Spaces**: Spaces are organizational containers that allow you to group related threads and files for a specific project or topic. This is particularly useful for Enterprise users who want to create dedicated knowledge bases for their work.[^2_3][^2_4][^2_5]

Perplexity offers granular control over what sources are included in your searches, particularly for Pro and Enterprise users.[^2_6]

* **Web Search**: You can toggle web search on or off at the beginning of a new thread. When turned off, Perplexity will only use its internal knowledge to answer your questions, without browsing the web for external sources.[^2_2]

# what about context across spaces?

No, context is not shared across different Spaces in Perplexity. Each Space functions as a self-contained and isolated environment for your research, threads, and files.[^4_1][^4_2]

### How Context is Scoped

* **Context within a Thread**: A single thread maintains the conversational context of all the exchanges within it. This allows you to ask follow-up questions and explore a topic in-depth, as the AI remembers what was previously discussed in that specific conversation.[^4_3][^4_4]
* **Context within a Space**: A Space is designed to organize threads and files related to a specific project or topic. The "context" for a Space is established by the files you upload to it and the instructions you provide. Any thread created *within that Space* can then use those uploaded files as a knowledge base for its answers. However, this context is strictly limited to that particular Space.[^4_5][^4_6][^4_7][^4_1]


### Isolation Between Spaces

The primary purpose of Spaces is to keep research organized and separate. For example:[^4_1]

* A Space you create for **"Founding a Business"** might contain your business plan, legal documents, and market research. Threads within this Space can draw information from these files.[^4_6][^4_1]
* A separate Space for **"Hobby Projects"** would be completely independent. It would not have any knowledge of the files or conversations happening within the "Founding a Business" Space, and vice-versa.[^4_2][^4_1]

This separation ensures that your research remains organized and that the information within one project does not unintentionally influence the answers in another.[^4_2][^4_1]

# or search across spaces ? can you link spaces? or seraching threads within a space, or would you make a thread a file in that space, and then its searchable for rest of space, but ONLY that space?

You cannot search across or link different Spaces; they are designed as separate, self-contained environments. However, you can search for threads within a single Space, and queries made within that Space can reference the files uploaded to it.[^5_1][^5_2][^5_3][^5_4]

### Search Capabilities and Limitations

* **No Cross-Space Search**: There is no feature that allows you to perform a single search query across multiple Spaces simultaneously. Each Space acts as an isolated container for its own threads and files. For Enterprise Pro users, it is possible to search an organization's entire internal knowledge base, which can centralize information, but this is distinct from searching across multiple, separate Spaces.[^5_3][^5_4][^5_1]
* **No Linking Spaces**: You cannot link different Spaces together. Their purpose is to keep projects and topics distinctly organized.[^5_1]


### Searching Within a Single Space

Within a given Space, you have two primary search functions:

1. **Finding Threads**: You can search for specific threads that have been added to the Space by using keywords or titles in the search bar, similar to how you search your main Library.[^5_5]
2. **Querying Files**: When you start a new thread inside a Space, you can ask questions directly about the content of the files (like PDFs, documents, etc.) that have been uploaded to that Space. Perplexity will use those files as a primary source to generate answers, and can enrich them with information from the web if you choose.[^5_2][^5_6][^5_4][^5_3]

### Threads as Searchable Content

You don't convert a thread into a file to make it searchable. Instead, you **add** an existing thread from your library to a Space. Once a thread is part of a Space, it is organized and can be found there. However, the conversational content of that thread does not automatically become a searchable knowledge source for *other* new threads in the way an uploaded file does. The search function within a Space is for finding the threads themselves or for querying the uploaded file repository.[^5_7][^5_2][^5_5][^5_1]

You're absolutely right—we discussed the *concept* and *power* of the parable, but we haven't actually crafted it yet as a standalone story. Let me address all your questions.

***

## The Parable: The Key and the Hidden Doors

**A researcher joined a new company and needed to solve a critical problem quickly. They knew their industry had specialized tools and resources, but couldn't find them through normal searches. Generic queries returned surface-level results that everyone already knew.**

**One day, a mentor said: "You're looking for doors, but you don't have the keys. In every field, the real knowledge is locked behind specific language—terminology that only insiders use. Find the right words, and the hidden doors will reveal themselves."**

**The researcher began listening carefully to how experts spoke. They noted unusual phrases, specific feature names, and technical terms. When they used these exact words in their searches, suddenly, targeted resources appeared—help docs, advanced features, and communities they never knew existed.**

**The lesson: Precision in language is the key. Once you speak the industry's dialect, you unlock doors to knowledge that competition can't find. This isn't just about "talking the talk"—it's about resonating at the right frequency so the solutions reveal themselves to you.**

***

## 1. Elevator Pitch Framework

Use the **Problem-Solution-Benefit-Call** structure:[^12_1][^12_2]

**"You know how [audience] struggles with [problem]? We've built [solution] that helps them [benefit] so they can [desired outcome]. [Question or next step]?"**

**Example for your vision:**
"You know how development teams struggle to prioritize endless feature requests and bug reports? We've built an AI orchestrator that automatically analyzes user feedback, prioritizes fixes by impact, and presents vetted recommendations to developers—so they can ship what matters most, faster. Want to see how it works?"[^12_2][^12_1]

**The three C's:** Clear, Concise, Compelling.[^12_3]

***

## 2. Framework for Prompt Alignment with Product Goals

Create a **prompt governance framework** with these components:

**Core Elements:**

- **Purpose anchor**: Every prompt must state the business objective it serves[^12_4]
- **Success criteria**: Define measurable outcomes (e.g., "reduce search time by 40%")[^12_4]
- **User persona context**: Include who will use it and their expertise level[^12_5]
- **Constraints**: List what must NOT be included (PII, proprietary data, off-brand tone)[^12_6]

**Review checklist:**

- Does this prompt serve a defined product goal?[^12_7]
- Is the expected output format clear?[^12_4]
- Have we tested it with edge cases?[^12_8]

***

## 3. Watchdog Sentinel Design for AI Agent Safety

Build a **multi-layered security architecture**:[^12_9][^12_10]

**Layer 1: Authentication \& Authorization**

- Cryptographic credentials for agent identity verification[^12_9]
- Role-based access control (RBAC) limiting what each agent can do[^12_10]

**Layer 2: Sandboxing \& Isolation**

- Agents run in isolated environments with minimal permissions[^12_9]
- Tool access restricted to specific, approved APIs[^12_10]

**Layer 3: Real-Time Monitoring**

- Log all agent actions with timestamps and change hashes[^12_10]
- Anomaly detection algorithms flag unusual behavior patterns[^12_9][^12_10]
- Automatic circuit breakers pause agents exhibiting risky behavior[^12_10]

**Layer 4: Human-in-the-Loop**

- High-risk actions require approval workflows[^12_10]
- Quarantine protocol for suspicious agents pending architect review[^12_10]

***

## 4. Auto-Prioritizing Dev Fix Orchestrator

**Steps to build:**

**Phase 1: Data Collection**

- Aggregate feedback from: user reports, system logs, usage analytics, support tickets[^12_4]
- Tag issues with metadata: severity, affected users, feature area[^12_6]

**Phase 2: AI Analysis**

- Deploy specialized agents to analyze each issue type[^12_6]
- Score issues on: business impact, technical complexity, user sentiment[^12_7]

**Phase 3: Recommendation Engine**

- Consolidate scores into weighted priority rankings[^12_4]
- Attach estimates: time to fix, resources needed, dependencies[^12_7]
- Generate impact reports: "Fixing X will improve retention by Y%"[^12_11]

**Phase 4: Developer Interface**

- Present vetted recommendations in a dashboard[^12_11]
- Enable one-click approval or rejection with feedback loop[^12_12]
- Track execution and automatically update stakeholders[^12_13]

***

## 5. Measuring User Adoption and Evangelism

**Key Metrics**:[^12_14][^12_15]

**Adoption Metrics:**

- **Product Adoption Rate**: (New Active Users / Total Signups) × 100[^12_14]
- **Time-to-First Key Action**: How quickly new users complete critical tasks[^12_14]
- **Feature Adoption Rate**: Percentage using specific features[^12_15]
- **Daily/Weekly Active Users (DAU/WAU)**: Engagement frequency[^12_15]

**Evangelism Indicators:**

- **Net Promoter Score (NPS)**: "How likely are you to recommend this?"[^12_15]
- **User-Generated Content**: Feature requests, forum posts, tutorial creation
- **Referral Rate**: How many users invite colleagues[^12_15]
- **Support Ticket Sentiment**: Shift from complaints to feature requests[^12_12]

**Advanced Tracking:**

- Cohort analysis: Compare adoption curves for different user groups[^12_15]
- Behavior flow mapping: Identify where users find value vs. drop off[^12_14]

# so if a thread is saved as a file to a space, is then ONLY that space/thread can see it? its not included in all ORG files? are those ONLY files I upload?

You are absolutely correct to ask for this clarification, as it highlights the crucial difference between project-level knowledge and organization-wide knowledge within Perplexity Enterprise.

When a thread is saved as a file to a Space, it is **only visible and searchable within that specific Space**. It is **not** automatically included in the broader "Organization Files" repository.[^13_1][^13_2]

These are two distinct tiers of file storage and knowledge management.

### Two Tiers of Knowledge: Spaces vs. Organization Files

Here’s a clear breakdown of the difference:


| Feature | **Files in a Space** | **Organization File Repository** |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Scope \& Visibility** | Private to that specific Space. Only members of the Space can search and use these files [^13_2][^13_1]. | Public to everyone in the entire organization. Appears under the "Org Files" source for all users [^13_3]. |
| **Who Can Add Files?** | Any member invited to the Space can typically upload files or save threads to it [^13_4]. | Primarily managed by **Administrators**. Admins can grant permission to other members to upload/delete files [^13_3][^13_5]. |
| **Primary Use Case** | Building a deep, focused knowledge base for a specific project, team, or topic [^13_6][^13_1]. | Creating a centralized, authoritative source of truth for company-wide documents, policies, and data [^13_3]. |

### Who Manages Organization Files?

To answer your last question, "Org Files" are **not** just files you personally upload. The Organization File Repository is a centralized library intended for company-wide resources. Your organization's administrators are the ones who primarily upload and manage the files in this repository to ensure the information is official and relevant to everyone.[^13_3][^13_5]

Think of it this way:

* A **Space** is like your team's private project binder.
* The **Organization Repository** is like the official company library, curated by the librarians (admins).
